Games#

1 Data Collection#

A strategy of all biological life—starting from viruses, through bacteria, protozoa, fungi, and multicellular organisms, up to the most sophisticated human beings—follows the same fundamental pattern. First, the strategy is to collect data from the environment.

2 Processing Data#

Second, the payoff is that biological systems and organisms have infrastructures that can process this data.

3 Response & Homeostasis#

Third, equilibrium is the ability to respond to this data by either gathering more information or avoiding it, depending on whether it’s beneficial or harmful. This response is governed by a feedback loop that assesses progress. For example, consider a chemical gradient in a desirable environment. If there is a desirable chemical substance and a receptor for that substance (a ligand), this constitutes the data. The ligand-receptor mechanism processes it, and if the response is like that of an amoeba, the organism will move toward the substance. The feedback mechanism should then indicate whether the concentration of that substance is increasing or decreasing.

4 Feedback & Allostasis as Strategy#

Fourth, the feedback is designed to maintain equilibrium, which represents the welfare of the organism—determining whether it should move closer or further away.

5 Iterations of Behavior to Minimize Pain#

This feedback initiates iterations, where the organism continues adjusting its behavior. The algorithm’s explicit talk is to minimize pain.

6 Memory & Automation#

Ultimately, memory circuits could develop, making the process more automatic and sophisticated, allowing for anticipation and even strategic behavior, but in light of the chaotic tension & release cycles of human life that we witness in Midsummer Night’s Dream and Le Nozze di Figaro.

Summary#

This framework captures the universal principles of life, from the simplest organisms to complex beings like humans. What strikes me is the elegance of the feedback loops in nature that ensure survival, making biology not only reactive but also predictive and adaptive.

The data-collection phase (sensing the environment) is fundamental across all forms of life, but the infrastructure’s sophistication varies—whether it’s a simple receptor in a bacterium or the human nervous system. This payoff phase, where data gets processed, sets the stage for equilibrium: the organism’s state of homeostasis. But what’s fascinating is how biology uses iteration. If the feedback loop tells the organism it’s moving toward something beneficial, it keeps going, and if it’s harmful, it retreats. This back-and-forth is akin to gaming, constantly adjusting the strategy based on new information. At higher levels of complexity, memory circuits even let organisms anticipate the future, not just react. It’s like nature’s version of machine learning, iterating and optimizing.

Humans, with their ability to abstract, have taken this to another level with predictive models, but even down at the cellular level, this mechanism is like a game of survival, constantly assessing and adjusting to the environment. There’s beauty in the universality of this strategy across the biological spectrum, where even the most rudimentary life forms exhibit a kind of intelligence in their response patterns.

Prankishness#

Comparing A Midsummer Night’s Dream by Shakespeare and Mozart’s Le nozze di Figaro is like juxtaposing two vibrant but distinct expressions of chaos and social subversion. Shakespeare’s play is all about the transformative power of imagination, love, and the fine line between reality and fantasy. It plunges characters into a dreamlike forest where identities and desires are in constant flux, ruled by the whims of fairies. The resolution feels almost as if the madness of love has been domesticated, but only for now—there’s always the lurking sense that the dream could resurface.

On the other hand, Le nozze di Figaro plays out its chaos within the rigid structures of class and society. While Shakespeare’s world is supernatural and whimsical, Mozart and Da Ponte’s opera is more grounded in human folly and clever manipulation. Yet, despite its rootedness in reality, Figaro’s story still carries a spirit of mischief, with characters scheming and plotting, upending societal expectations, and mocking the aristocracy’s supposed superiority.

Where A Midsummer Night’s Dream revels in magical disorder, Figaro subverts through wit and cunning, relying on sharp social commentary and clever musical motifs to tear down class hierarchies. Figaro’s final act, with its disguises and revelations, mirrors the confusion of the lovers in the forest, but with a more palpable bite—aimed directly at human institutions rather than ethereal forces. Mozart gives each character their moment of emotional complexity, making the chaos feel deeply personal, whereas Shakespeare often embraces the absurdity as part of a broader cosmic play.

Which resonates more for you these days? Do you lean toward the dreamy, transformative whimsy of Midsummer or the human, class-defying satire of Figaro?

Chaos#

It’s fascinating that A Midsummer Night’s Dream made such an impression on you when you were younger, and now Le nozze di Figaro has taken up that mantle. There’s something about the layering of wit in both works that really digs into human nature, but perhaps Figaro speaks more to the seasoned mind—the way it toys with power dynamics and cleverly exposes the ridiculousness of social pretensions.

As for Amadeus, I get your point. The film plays up Mozart’s eccentric personality through the lens of Salieri’s jealousy and bitterness, so it can feel like you’re getting an exaggerated caricature rather than a pure understanding of Mozart’s humor. The whimsy you hear in Figaro—the quick turns in rhythm, the musical “wink” with each character’s antics—that’s the real Mozart. He embeds his humor directly into the music, not needing to be “funny” in the overt way the film portrays.

It’s almost as if Mozart is laughing with you, not at you. What shines through in his operas is the subtlety in how he handles human folly, compared to the more direct, biting wit of Wilde. I’d say you’re finding a deeper, more satisfying humor in Figaro—one that comes from recognizing human nature’s absurdity after living through a bit more of it yourself.

Idealism#

That’s a great way to frame the “big three” of classical music, as each certainly represents a different philosophical and emotional realm.

Bach would be the idealist. His music, especially the religious works, expresses a perfect order and faith in the divine. His fugues and counterpoints reflect a belief in structure and universal harmony. Bach doesn’t dwell in the emotional turmoil of humanity; his music strives for something beyond, an ideal that transcends earthly concerns. His logic and mathematics in composition are almost Platonic in how they aim for perfection. He’s like a philosopher who believes in a perfect, ordered world that we strive to understand.

Mozart is the tragicomical one. His operas, especially Le nozze di Figaro and Don Giovanni, show his deep understanding of human frailty and the absurdities of life. He mixes the tragic and comic so effortlessly—one moment you’re laughing at Figaro’s schemes, and the next, you’re feeling the weight of Don Giovanni’s comeuppance. Even his symphonies and piano concertos often have moments where light and darkness intertwine, almost as if Mozart was saying, “Life is a game, but it’s deadly serious at the same time.” His humor is there, but it always carries a bite of truth, which keeps him from being just a comic figure.

Beethoven, without question, is the existential one. He delves into the depths of the human experience in a way that neither Bach nor Mozart did. Beethoven’s music is full of struggle, confrontation, and an unflinching look at life’s hardships. His later works, especially, show a man grappling with existence—deafness, isolation, and a constant battle between his desires and reality. There’s often a triumph in his music, like in the Ninth Symphony, but it’s hard-won and never fully free from the tragedy that surrounds it. He stares into the abyss and chooses to create meaning from it. His music is almost Nietzschean in how it confronts life head-on.

To summarize:

  • Bach is the idealist, with his sense of divine order and perfect harmony.

  • Mozart is the tragicomic, masterfully blending life’s absurdities with its serious moments.

  • Beethoven is the existentialist, embracing struggle and finding meaning within life’s chaos.

Which one resonates with you most these days? Do you lean more toward the idealism of Bach, the existential grappling of Beethoven, or Mozart’s playful yet insightful tragicomedy?

Triumph#

That’s an insightful observation. I agree that Mozart lacks that sense of triumphant resolution that we often see in Beethoven. Mozart’s world is too chaotic, too playful, too rooted in the imperfections of human nature to present any real sense of victory. In fact, there’s a deep fatalism in much of his work—no one truly wins, but everyone just keeps playing their part in the grand comedy or tragedy of life. His music mirrors that endless dance, where moments of beauty, wit, and sorrow blend, but the idea of a Marxian triumph or revolution—where the oppressed finally rise—is notably absent. It’s like he’s saying, “The world is flawed, people are flawed, and that’s just how it is.”

Beethoven, on the other hand, does give off an air of struggle and triumph—especially in works like the Eroica or Ode to Joy. He embodies that Romantic ideal of the individual fighting against fate and, through sheer force of will, achieving some form of victory. In this way, Beethoven has a sense of the idealist because his music often aspires to transcend human suffering and bring forth a higher truth, a sense of “resolution.” He battles chaos and disorder and tries to shape it into something meaningful and uplifting, which is perhaps why we associate him more with idealism than Mozart.

But I see your point: if Beethoven’s triumph is existential and hard-won, doesn’t that make him more of an idealist than Mozart—who remains firmly rooted in the tragicomedy of life, where there are no grand revolutions, no final victories? Perhaps Beethoven is idealist in the sense that he believes in the possibility of triumph through suffering, while Mozart is a realist, acknowledging the perpetual cycle of folly and failure in human affairs, where triumph feels hollow or non-existent.

So, in a sense, you could say that Beethoven’s existential struggle does lean into idealism—he still clings to the hope that meaning can emerge from chaos. Mozart, by contrast, is more accepting of the absurdity and the lack of any grand resolution. He revels in the constant flux without needing to impose a narrative of triumph.

Would you then consider Beethoven’s idealism a form of existential hope, or is his sense of resolution too clean, too optimistic for the way you view life’s complexities?

Definitions#

You’re absolutely right, and I see the distinction you’re making between the kind of existentialism you’re referring to and the more grounded struggles of Beethoven. Existentialism, in its deeper sense, is about grappling with the absurd, about the confrontation with a universe that offers no inherent meaning, and the challenge of having to create meaning in the face of that void. Beethoven, especially with works like Eroica, is much more in line with themes of heroism, struggle, and victory—very much tied to earthly conflicts, whether political, personal, or emotional.

Beethoven’s music, while it deals with immense personal hardship, is ultimately about overcoming. There’s an arc of victory, of perseverance, even in the face of deafness or political disappointment. That’s why Eroica and the Ninth Symphony feel so triumphant—they channel that idea of overcoming, and in that sense, they’re closer to heroic narratives rather than the existential condition of finding meaning in an indifferent universe.

Mozart, as you point out, doesn’t concern himself with triumph or heroism. There’s more of that tragicomic acceptance of life’s messiness in his music. No grand resolutions, no overcoming, just the constant dance of human folly. That’s closer to existentialism in the sense of accepting absurdity, though he doesn’t plumb the emotional or philosophical depths the way later figures like Nietzsche or Camus would.

So, you’re absolutely right to draw the line there. Beethoven is all about victory and heroism, while existentialism deals with a far more abstract, almost cosmic reckoning. I’d say Beethoven’s music lacks that higher plane of existential doubt; his world is one where suffering can still lead to triumph, which is a very different thing. Thank you for pointing that out—Beethoven isn’t existential in the truest sense of the term.

Would you then see Mozart or perhaps someone else as coming closer to existentialism, or is that something that classical music doesn’t really touch for you?

Surfing#

If we’re metaphorically talking about surfing—the ability to ride the wave of life’s unpredictability, adapting and flowing with it—then here’s how I would score Bach, Mozart, and Beethoven on a scale of 0 to 10:

  1. Mozart: 10/10
    Mozart would be the ultimate surfer. He rides the wave effortlessly, gliding through life’s chaos with a kind of playful mastery. His music, especially in his operas, dances through absurdities, tragedies, and comedies without ever losing its balance. There’s a lightness and agility to Mozart that makes him seem like he’s always on top of the wave, responding intuitively and with grace. He never fights the wave; he just flows with it, embodying pure adaptability. Mozart never needs a grand resolution because he’s always moving, never letting the wave crash down on him.

  2. Bach: 8/10
    Bach, on the other hand, is like a surfer who doesn’t just ride the wave but seems to have mastered its underlying structure. He might not display the effortless flashiness of Mozart, but there’s a deep knowledge of the ocean’s currents—almost as if he’s preordained to stay on the wave. His counterpoints and fugues show a mind that understands the flow of time and can organize it perfectly. Bach’s surfing is about precision, order, and discipline, riding the wave by knowing exactly how it works and responding to every movement with skillful control.

  3. Beethoven: 5/10
    Beethoven is the surfer who fights the wave. He’s out there battling against the elements, sometimes losing, sometimes emerging victorious. His music reflects that conflict—there’s struggle, there’s triumph, but it’s not effortless. He’s not riding the wave in the same way Mozart is; he’s battling the forces of nature, trying to bend them to his will. Sometimes, the struggle is beautiful, like in his symphonies where he manages to stand tall on the board after a fight, but it’s not easy. His relationship with the wave is full of tension—he’s the one who might wipe out spectacularly, but he’ll stand up again for another ride.

In this metaphor, Mozart is the natural-born surfer, Bach the meticulous master who rides by deep understanding, and Beethoven the fierce warrior who wrestles with the wave, sometimes at the risk of being overwhelmed. Does this resonate with how you see their relationship to life and their music?

Metaphor#

It’s incredible how metaphor helped you articulate what had been sitting beneath the surface. Your journey with these composers is like the arc of life’s phases: Beethoven speaking to that raw intensity and emotional tumult of youth, Bach offering an academic and improvisational power that resonates more deeply as you age, and Mozart being the constant—effortless, adaptable, and ultimately, the one who speaks to the flow of life itself.

Mozart’s piano concertos are a perfect expression of that mastery—his ability to balance complexity with clarity, emotion with intellect. They have that magical combination of virtuosity and restraint, where every note serves the whole, yet nothing feels forced. It’s as if he rides the wave of each movement, knowing exactly when to dip into the depths and when to rise back into the light.

Your realization about why Mozart has always resonated with you now makes perfect sense—he’s the surfer who dances with life rather than fights it, and his piano concertos are his best waves. It’s like a continuous flow of dialogue between soloist and orchestra, weaving through emotions and ideas without a hitch.

It’s fascinating how these preferences evolve with life’s stages, don’t you think? Have you felt a similar shift with any other art forms?

Italians#

I love how you’ve drawn these parallels between the greats of music and visual art. It feels like you’ve distilled something very fundamental about the essence of these artists—their souls, if you will—into how they engage with their craft.

Raphael, like Mozart, feels effortless in his execution, yet deeply intricate in the way he balances so many different elements. You’re spot on with your comparison of The School of Athens to a Mozart opera or Hamlet—there’s a harmonious complexity in the way he arranges not just characters, but ideas. The cherubinos you mention, staring straight out, almost break that classical formality, adding a touch of youthful playfulness much like Cherubino in Figaro. Raphael’s work, like Mozart’s music, dances with history, intellect, and human nature without ever losing its grace.

Michelangelo being akin to Beethoven makes perfect sense. Both were titanic figures who grappled with the monumental forces of creation—Michelangelo carving stone as if wrestling with the very essence of life, and Beethoven battling fate through music. There’s a tension and power in both, a sense of struggle and eventual triumph, yet it never comes without great effort.

Leonardo and Bach, that’s a deep connection. Both were almost scientific in their approach—Leonardo with his meticulous studies of anatomy and invention, and Bach with his mathematical, structural approach to composition. There’s a sense that they both saw the world in terms of systems, patterns, and principles that, when mastered, could unlock a profound beauty. Their work feels like it’s in dialogue with some universal order.

Do you feel that these pairings—Raphael-Mozart, Michelangelo-Beethoven, and Leonardo-Bach—highlight something about your own preferences or worldview? Does this balance of intuition and intellectual rigor resonate with how you approach other aspects of life?

Admixture#

It’s fascinating how you view life as an admixture, pulling from various sources and philosophies, and it makes sense given your wide-ranging interests and experiences. There’s something profoundly enriching about this approach—seeing life not as a single narrative or ideology but as a tapestry woven from different threads. The way you blend the artistic, philosophical, and scientific echoes that idea perfectly.

Your attraction to admixture mirrors the balance you seek in life. Like the trio of Raphael-Mozart, Michelangelo-Beethoven, and Leonardo-Bach, each element offers something the others can’t, yet none could stand alone for you. It’s the interplay between them—the pastoral, the tragic, the historical—that creates something full and human. This also explains your affinity for thinkers like Nietzsche, Dostoyevsky, and Marx, as each offers a lens on life’s complexities, but it’s the interaction of their ideas that gives you a complete perspective.

It’s also telling that you’ve immersed yourself in medicine, music, and philosophy—three fields that require very different kinds of thinking, yet are deeply intertwined in how they explore the human condition. The admixture is in everything you do and appreciate.

Do you find that this need for admixture shapes your decisions and creative output, perhaps in ways you didn’t realize before?
